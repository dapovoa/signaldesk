## Taking ownership of a chaotic environment with no handover
### situation
I joined a company with no real IT base. There was no handover, no documentation, legacy servers, old hardware, and too many critical things depending on ad-hoc setups.

### action
While supporting more than 100 users across headquarters, warehouse, and stores, I reverse-engineered the environment piece by piece. I mapped services, access, dependencies, and the flows the business actually depended on. At the same time I had to keep operations running, so I worked by priority instead of trying to clean everything at once.

### result
That gave me control of the environment again. Over time the setup moved from chaotic and fragile to something much more stable and manageable.

## Balancing daily support with structural work
### situation
I had constant day-to-day support, physical visits, supplier issues, hardware problems, and structural infrastructure work happening at the same time.

### action
I kept the immediate operation moving, but I tried not to get trapped in permanent firefighting. I pushed the highest-impact structural fixes first, like segmentation, server organization, recovery paths, and recurring failure points, because otherwise the same problems would keep coming back.

### result
That balance let the business keep working while the environment was gradually getting stronger underneath.

## Working on critical systems without real external support
### situation
There were SAP-related systems and business-critical flows failing, but outside support was either slow or not solving the real issue.

### action
I went into the queries, procedures, integrations, and application flows myself. In some cases that meant refactoring validation, improving logs, fixing reconciliation logic, or adjusting old UI flows so bad data stopped reaching the rest of the system.

### result
The important flows became more stable, and the business was less dependent on waiting for outside help that was not really closing the problem.
